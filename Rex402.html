How can cold light technology improve city designs?
  • Efficiency: Bioluminescence is "cold light," meaning it loses almost no energy as heat.
  • Sustainability: Mimicking this could create streetlights that use a fraction of the electricity of modern bulbs, reducing carbon emissions.

Unit 2A: Defense
5. Burglar Alarm
Explain the biological "burglar alarm" effect.
  • Organism: Dinoflagellates (plankton).
  • Trigger: When disturbed by a predator (like a shrimp), they flash.
  • Result: The flash attracts a larger predator (fish) to eat the shrimp, saving the plankton.
Reading Skill: Understanding Metaphor.
HOTS: Analysis (Deconstructing complex defense mechanisms).
Unit 2A: Physics
6. Blue Light Physics
Why is the ocean's blue light the last color absorbed?
  • Wavelength: Water molecules absorb long wavelengths (red) quickly.
  • Penetration: Short wavelengths (blue) travel through water molecules easily, creating the "perpetual twilight" mentioned in Paragraph C.
Reading Skill: Identifying Cause and Effect.
HOTS: Analysis (Scientific reasoning).
Unit 2A: Adaptation
7. Depth & Color
How does ocean depth affect an animal's glowing light color?
  • Matching: Since only blue light exists deep down, most animals (squid, jellyfish) glow blue to communicate.
  • Camouflage: Deep sea creatures are often red. Since no red light exists to reflect off them, they appear black/invisible.

Synthesis: Evolution
10. Evolution Synthesis
Both the Firefly and the Bird of Paradise make themselves highly visible. Evaluate this trade-off.
  • Risk: Visibility attracts predators (e.g., bright feathers in a green forest).
  • Reward: Visibility attracts mates.
  • Result: Sexual selection favors reproduction over longevity. It is better to live short and reproduce than live long and die alone.
Reading Skill: Synthesizing across units (2A & 2B).
HOTS: Evaluation (Weighing evolutionary costs and benefits).
Ecology: Impact
11. Pollution Impact
How might artificial light (submarines) disrupt deep-sea communication?
  • Signal Swamping: Deep sea animals rely on faint flashes to find mates. Bright submersible lights would blind them or wash out their signals.
  • Defense Failure: Counter-illumination (hiding shadows) fails if the ambient light is artificial and unpredictable.
Reading Skill: Application; Predicting Consequences.
HOTS: Analysis (Impact of technology).

What changes might stop the chemical reaction in Bio Bays?

Ecology: Food Webs
14. Food Web Crash
How does the loss of the "burglar alarm" plankton affect the food web?
  • Bottom-Up Effect: Plankton are the base of the food chain. If they die, primary consumers starve.
  • Predator Blindness: Without the "alarm," intermediate predators (shrimp) might overpopulate because larger predators can't find them in the dark.
Reading Skill: Inferring Consequences.
HOTS: Analysis (Ecological systems).
Ecology: Invasive Species
15. Invasive Species
What advantage would a bioluminescent species have in a non-glowing lake?
  • Super-Stimulus: Local mates might be attracted to the "super-normal" bright lights, ignoring their own species.
  • Lack of Predators: Local predators might fear the light (thinking it is toxic) or not know how to hunt it.
Reading Skill: Predicting.
HOTS: Synthesis (Applying knowledge to new contexts).

20. Future Adaptation
If the ocean becomes murkier (pollution), will bioluminescence fail?
  • Physics: Light cannot travel through murky (turbid) water.
  • Adaptation: Animals might have to rely on lateral lines (vibration) or smell instead of light signals, or die out.
