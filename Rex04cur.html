Unit 1: Images of Life

Can we trust what we see?
"Does the frame create the truth?"
1A: Perceiving • 1B: Empathizing
1A: The Visual Village
Perceiving
"Generate the area 'outside the frame' of a crowded protest photo. Show what was excluded."
Use: controversy, profound, realistic, represent, tremendous
Writing Prompt
Part 1: Analysis
Explain the controversy surrounding the use of filters to edit news photos. Does editing make an image more dramatic and profound, or does it fail to represent the truth instantly? Part 2: Personal Connection
Describe a specific photo you saw on your own social media feed recently that seemed obsessed with perfection. Did you feel the image was profound or fake?
Warm-Up Show a "Beauty Filter" app. Ask: "If you meet this person in real life, are they a liar? Or just 'enhanced'?"
Prompt "If a war photographer removes a bloody object from a photo to make it less gruesome, is that an act of kindness or a lie?"
Support "It is a lie because the photo needs to be realistic to show the truth."
Extension "While the intention might be to protect the viewer, altering the image creates a controversy about what the photo truly represents."
Hypothetical "What if removing the blood helps the photo get published on the front page, alerting the world to the war? Does the outcome justify the edit?"
1B: Journey in Photos
Empathizing
"Write a diary entry describing the sensation of ice and fear for children."
Use: enrich, expectation, overcome, sincere, portrait
Writing Prompt
Part 1: Analysis
Describe how a photographer must overcome the obsession with getting a perfect shot to form a sincere connection. How does teamwork help a project turn out successfully? Part 2: Personal Connection
Describe a "quiet moment" or portrait you took of a friend. Was it hard to get a sincere reaction, or did you have to embrace the moment?
Warm-Up Play a sound clip of a storm. Ask: "How would you photograph 'Fear' without showing a face?"
Prompt "Is it ethical to take a portrait of a stranger who is suffering, just to win an award?"
Support "It is wrong because the photographer is putting their project above the person."
Extension "However, if the photo is sincere and helps the world understand the suffering, the photographer has overcome the ethical problem."
Hypothetical "What if the subject begs you not to take the photo, but you know the photo could stop the war? Do you click the shutter?"
GRASPS Task: Visual Literacy
Goal: Establish a new ethical standard for digital photography.
Role: Chief Ethics Editor for National Geographic
Audience: Board of Directors & Junior Photographers
Situation: A prize-winning photo has been flagged as "digitally altered" (e.g. a pole removed). Decide if it is Truth or Deception.
Product: A "Code of Ethics" Manifesto and a verdict on the photo.

Unit 3: Food & Health

How safe is our food?
"Are we redefining what it means to be 'natural'?"
3A: Synthesizing • 3B: Abstracting
3A: The Super-Label
Synthesizing
"Design a food warning label showing the risk of contamination and explaining that washing is compulsory."
Use: contamination, compulsory, integral, nationwide, optimistic
Writing Prompt
Part 1: Analysis
Explain why it is integral to determine the source of an infection nationwide. Is it feasible to keep food completely safe, or will unseen bacteria always disturb our digestion? Part 2: Personal Connection
Describe a specific local restaurant or school cafeteria you visit. Do you feel optimistic about their hygiene? Have you ever had an issue with digestion?
Warm-Up "Imagine a tomato that never rots. It stays red for 10 years. Do you eat it?"
Prompt "If a Genetically Modified (GM) crop can end world hunger, but might contaminate wild plants, should we plant it?"
Support "We should plant it because feeding people is feasible and necessary."
Extension "Even if we represent an optimistic view on hunger, we cannot determine the long-term damage if the genes infect the wild ecosystem."
Hypothetical "What if a company owns the patent to the 'Killer Corn' and forbids farmers from saving seeds? Is the food worth the loss of freedom?"
3B: GMO Metaphor
Abstracting
"Explain gene modification without biological terms, using a 'Lego' metaphor."
Use: allergic, conventional, diminish, modify, revolution
Writing Prompt
Part 1: Analysis
If we modify a plant's traits, might we accidentally create a new allergic reaction or infect the environment? Is there a safe alternative? Part 2: Personal Connection
Go to your kitchen and find a food item with a label. Is it conventional or modified? Do you check labels compulsorily?
Warm-Up "Germs are bad. True or False? (False - we need them)."
Prompt "Should the government ban raw milk to protect safety, or allow people to choose their own nutritional risks?"
Support "The government should ban it to prevent a deficiency in safety standards."
Extension "People should have an alternative choice; relying solely on conventional pasteurized milk ignores the potential benefits of raw bacteria."
Hypothetical "What if science proves that being 'too clean' causes allergies? Should we make it mandatory for children to play in the dirt?"

12A: The Goldilocks Zone
Modeling
"Visualize a planet where plants thrive. Atmosphere resembles Earth's."
Use: atmosphere, composition, resemble, scope, thrive
Writing Prompt
Part 1: Analysis
Scientists analyze atomic composition to find planets that resemble Earth. Hence, if we find a planet where life can thrive, should we exclude moving there given Earth's toxic state? Part 2: Personal Connection
If you found a planet resembling Earth but with no pollution, would you move there? Does the size of the universe make you feel small or excited?
Warm-Up "Look up. There is a rock moving 30,000 mph. We can't see it. Should we worry?"
Prompt "Should we spend billions to watch the sky for asteroids, or spend that money on poverty?"
Support "Spend it on space because an asteroid has a huge scope of damage."
Extension "We must spend it on space; poverty is bad, but an asteroid impact would change the composition of the atmosphere, making it impossible to thrive."
Hypothetical "What if we find an asteroid hitting in 50 years? Do we fix it now, or leave it for our children?"
12B: Asteroid Deflection
Abstracting
"Illustrate a scenario where we deflect a meteor to avoid a catastrophe."
Use: catastrophe, deflect, dilemma, scenario, smash
Writing Prompt
Part 1: Analysis
We face a dilemma: should we engage nuclear motors to deflect an asteroid, or will that cause a worse catastrophe? Would such an event change the atmospheric composition? Part 2: Personal Connection
How would your specific family react to this news? Who would panic, and who would try to solve it? Do you think humanity could thrive after such an event?
Warm-Up "We can nuke the asteroid. Like in the movies. Is that actually a good idea?"
Prompt "If an asteroid is coming, should we tell the public and cause panic, or keep it secret?"
Support "Keep it secret to avoid a catastrophe of panic."
Extension "Tell the public; engaging in a scenario where the government hides a dilemma like this creates mistrust."
Hypothetical "We can save Earth by using nuclear weapons in space. But if it fails, the radiation will kill us faster. Do we risk it?"
GRASPS Task: Planetary Defense
Goal: Save the planet from an existential threat.
Role: Planetary Defense Commander
Audience: UN Security Council
Situation: Asteroid predicted to smash into Earth. Choose: Deflect or Destroy?
Product: Crisis Action Plan comparing catastrophic risks vs cost.
