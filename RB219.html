Ocean Acidification: A Growing Threat to Marine Ecosystems

The rapid increase in atmospheric CO₂ over the past century has not only contributed to global warming but has also led to a phenomenon known as ocean acidification. As more carbon dioxide dissolves into seawater, the pH of the ocean declines, creating a more acidic environment. This shift in ocean chemistry can severely affect marine organisms, particularly those that rely on calcium carbonate to build shells and skeletons—such as corals, mollusks, and some species of plankton.

Numerous studies have shown that as ocean acidity increases, the ability of these organisms to form and maintain their structures diminishes, which in turn threatens entire ecosystems. Coral reefs, for example, are especially vulnerable; their degradation not only reduces biodiversity but also impacts coastal communities that depend on them for food, tourism, and coastal protection.

Scientists are actively researching mitigation strategies—from reducing carbon emissions at their source to exploring methods that might enhance the natural buffering capacity of seawater. While significant uncertainties remain about the long-term impacts, there is a growing consensus on the urgent need to address ocean acidification as part of a broader strategy for environmental sustainability.
